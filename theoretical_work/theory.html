Bayesian Hierarchical Model of Match Uncertainty

CSAS 2026

Author

Naren Prakash

Research Question

Goal: Compare statistical entropy (match uncertainty) between teams that would eventually be relegated vs. teams that were not.

Approach: Use bookmaker odds to compute Shannon entropy for each match, then model entropy as a function of eventual relegation status using a Bayesian hierarchical model.

Data Structure

Observations

  • Match \(i\) in season \(t\) between home team \(h\) and away team \(a\)
  • Bookmaker probabilities from Bet365 odds (power transformation)
  • Probability vector: \(p_i = (p_{i,w}, p_{i,d}, p_{i,l})\)
    • \(p_{i,w}\) = probability of home win
    • \(p_{i,d}\) = probability of draw
    • \(p_{i,l}\) = probability of away win (home loss)

Relegation/Promotion Indicators

  • \(R_{c,t} \in \{0, 1\}\) = 1 if team \(c\) is relegated at the end of season \(t\)
  • \(P_{c,t} \in \{0, 1\}\) = 1 if team \(c\) was promoted into season \(t\)

Note: Relegation status is known only after the season ends, but we use it to explain entropy patterns during the season.

Shannon Entropy (Match Uncertainty)

Definition

For match \(i\), entropy of the bookmaker probability distribution:

\[H_i = -\sum_{k \in \{w,d,l\}} p_{i,k} \log_3(p_{i,k})\]

Properties

  • Range: \(H_i \in [0, 1]\) (base-3 log normalizes to unit interval)
  • \(H_i = 0\): Certain outcome (one outcome near 100%)
  • \(H_i = 1\): Maximum uncertainty (equal probabilities: 1/3, 1/3, 1/3)

Bayesian Hierarchical Model

Likelihood

Since \(H_i \in [0, 1]\), we use Beta regression:

\[H_i \sim \text{Beta}(\mu_i, \phi)\]

where:

  • \(\mu_i \in [0, 1]\) = expected entropy for match \(i\)
  • \(\phi > 0\) = precision parameter (higher = less variance around mean)

Linear Predictor

\[\text{logit}(\mu_i) = \delta_0 + \delta_R (R_{h,t} + R_{a,t}) + \delta_P (P_{h,t} + P_{a,t}) + \theta_{h,t} + \theta_{a,t}\]

Parameters:

  • \(\delta_0\) = baseline entropy (neither relegated nor promoted)
  • \(\delta_R\) = effect of each relegated team in the match
  • \(\delta_P\) = effect of each promoted team in the match
  • \(\theta_{h,t}, \theta_{a,t}\) = team-specific random effects

Hierarchical Priors

Team random effects (partial pooling):

\[\theta_{c,t} \sim \text{Normal}(\mu_c, \sigma_\theta)\] \[\mu_c \sim \text{Normal}(0, \tau)\]

This allows each team to have its own entropy tendency, while borrowing strength across teams.

Hyperpriors

\[\delta_0 \sim \text{Normal}(0, 1)\] \[\delta_R \sim \text{Normal}(0, 0.5)\] \[\delta_P \sim \text{Normal}(0, 0.5)\] \[\sigma_\theta \sim \text{HalfCauchy}(0, 1)\] \[\tau \sim \text{HalfCauchy}(0, 1)\] \[\phi \sim \text{Gamma}(2, 0.1)\]

Hypotheses

Primary Hypothesis (Relegation)

H1: Matches involving eventually-relegated teams have different entropy than other matches.

  • \(\delta_R < 0\): Lower entropy — Relegated teams play predictable matches (blowout losses to stronger teams)
  • \(\delta_R > 0\): Higher entropy — Relegated teams play in chaotic, unpredictable matches
  • \(\delta_R \approx 0\): No difference — Bookmakers don’t distinguish relegated teams

Secondary Hypothesis (Promotion)

H2: Matches involving newly-promoted teams have different entropy.

  • \(\delta_P > 0\): Higher entropy — Bookmakers uncertain about promoted teams (unknown quantity)
  • \(\delta_P < 0\): Lower entropy — Promoted teams are clearly outclassed

Exploratory: Six-Pointers

Matches where both teams are relegated (\(R_{h,t} = 1\) AND \(R_{a,t} = 1\)):

  • These “six-pointers” may have unusually high entropy (anyone’s game)
  • Could add interaction term: \(\delta_{RR} \cdot (R_{h,t} \times R_{a,t})\)

Model Extensions

1. Asymmetric Home/Away Effects

Allow relegated home vs. away teams to have different effects:

\[\text{logit}(\mu_i) = \delta_0 + \delta_{R,h} R_{h,t} + \delta_{R,a} R_{a,t} + \delta_{P,h} P_{h,t} + \delta_{P,a} P_{a,t} + \theta_{h,t} + \theta_{a,t}\]

2. Time-Varying Relegation Effect

Test if entropy difference emerges over the season:

\[\delta_R(w) = \delta_{R,0} + \delta_{R,1} \cdot \text{match\_week}\]

  • Early season: \(\delta_R\) small (unclear who will go down)
  • Late season: \(\delta_R\) large (pattern obvious to bookmakers)

3. Dynamic Team Strength (Random Walk)

Track team entropy tendencies across seasons:

\[\theta_{c,t} \sim \text{Normal}(\theta_{c,t-1}, \sigma_{\text{drift}})\]

Useful if teams have persistent “chaotic” or “predictable” styles.

4. Opponent Quality Interaction

Relegated team vs. top-6 may differ from relegated team vs. mid-table:

\[+ \gamma \cdot (R_{h,t} \times \text{top6}_{a,t})\]

Implementation Notes

Software

  • brms package in R (Stan backend)
  • Family: Beta() with logit link
  • Syntax example:
brm(
  bf(entropy | trials(1) ~ relegated_count + promoted_count + (1 | team)),
  data = matches,
  family = Beta(),
  prior = c(
    prior(normal(0, 1), class = Intercept),
    prior(normal(0, 0.5), class = b),
    prior(cauchy(0, 1), class = sd)
  )
)

Diagnostics

  • \(\hat{R} < 1.05\) for all parameters (convergence)
  • Effective sample size > 400 per chain
  • Posterior predictive checks: Does simulated entropy match observed?

Data Requirements

  • Seasons: 2003-04 through 2024-25 (21 seasons)
  • Matches: ~8,000 total
  • Relegated teams: 3 per season × 21 = 63 team-seasons
  • Promoted teams: 3 per season × 21 = 63 team-seasons

Expected Contributions

  1. Methodological: Entropy as measure of bookmaker uncertainty in sports betting
  2. Substantive: Do bookmakers “know” which teams will be relegated early?
  3. Practical: Could entropy patterns inform early-season relegation prediction
